## Spelling:
-  All nouns in German are written with **capital** letters **regardless of location** in a sentence (Example: Der **Mann** und die **Frau** arbeiten jeden **Tag** am **Computer**)
- pronoun **ich** (I) has no initial capital, but **Sie** (you) has

## Different characters:
- **ß** is called es·tset, 
	- comes after **ei**, **ie**, or **a**, **o**, **u**
		- **heißen** - to be called
		- **Straße** - street
		- **groß** - big
- umlauts **ä**, **ö**, **ü**
	- change the pronunciation of a word and its meaning
	- **Mutter** is mother, but **Mütter** is mothers
	- **Mustte** means had to, but **Müstte** means should

## Three genders
- Nouns are either **masculine**, **feminine** or **neuter**
- articles: **der** for masculine, **die** for feminine, **das** for neuter
- Grammatical not biological
	- **der Tisch** - the table (masculine)
	- **die Tür** - the door (feminine)
	- **das Fenster** - the window (neuter)
-  Best to learn a new noun is with articles

## Endings
- German words take specific endings depending on their relationship to other parts of the sentence. 
- This applies to verbs, articles and possessive adjectives and adjectives
- Verbs
	- Describe the action of a sentence, such as "to run", "to think"
	- German "to go" has different endings when used with "I", "he" and "they"
		- Ich **geh*e*** - I go
		- Er **geh*t*** - He goes
		- Sie **geh*en*** - They go
- Articles and possessive adjectives
	- words are linked to a noun such as "a", "the", "my" or "his"
	- indefinite article "a" changes in German when it is linked to the subject of the sentence (**ein Mann**) or the object of the sentence (**ein*en*** Mann)
		- **Ist das ein Mann?** - Is that a man?
		- **Da drüben sehe ich einen Mann.** - I can see a man there.
- Adjectives
	- describe the quality of a noun
	- In German adjectives have different endings when they are linked to different genders
		- ein **neu*er*** Computer
		- eine **intteligent*e*** Frau
		- ein **schön*es*** Haus

## Cases
- You can tell what function a noun performs based on its ending and the form of the article
- A noun can be the subject of the sentence, the agent of what is happening
	- ***Der Hund* beißt den Mann.**
- Or it can be the object, the receiver of the action
	- **Der Hund beißt *den Mann***
- The subject and the object are in **different cases**, which means that the article has a **different ending**. 
- Both "dog" and "man" are masculine (**der**) but "the dog" (**der Hund**) is the subject and "the man" (**den Mann**) is the object.

## Word order
- Word order is flexible in German
- Position of the finite verb matters
	- The finite verb is *the second idea* in most statements:
		- Er ***hat*** zwei Brüder
		- Morgen ***fahre*** ich nach Manchester
	- Finite verbs goes the beginning of orders and questions:
		- ***Öffnet*** das Fenster!
		- ***Hast*** Du morge Zeit?
	- Finite verb goes at the end in subordinate clauses:
		- Ich kann morgen nicht kommen, weil ich nach Manchester ***fahre***.
	- If there are two verb forms, one of them goes at the end
		- Morgen **muss** ich nach Manchster **fahren**.

## Tenses
- In English tenses differentiate between an action happening at the moment (‘I am working’) and an action taking place regularly (‘I work at Harrods’)
- In German the finite verb form is the same in both statements
	- Ich **arbeite**
	- Ich **arbeite** bei Harrods
- Past tense in english is expressed either by present perfect tense or simple past tense
- German is simpler: normally use the present perfect when you talk about the past regardless of when it happend
- The simple past is used in written German
